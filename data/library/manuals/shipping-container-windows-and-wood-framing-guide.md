# Shipping Container Windows and Wood Framing Guide

This guide is a practical field note for adding framed openings and wood interior structure to a shipping container build.

## 1. Plan the opening before cutting

- Keep openings between corner castings and major structural rails when possible.
- Avoid stacking multiple large cuts close together without reinforcement.
- Measure interior finish thickness before choosing window rough-opening size.
- Mark the cut from both inside and outside so the frame lands where you expect.

## 2. Reinforce before or immediately after cutting

- A container wall is thin corrugated steel and loses stiffness fast once cut.
- For door and window openings, weld a rectangular steel tube frame around the opening.
- Common choices are square tubing or rectangular tubing sized to the load and opening.
- Tie the reinforcement into stronger structural members, not only sheet steel.

## 3. Build the rough opening

- Cut the opening slightly oversize only if needed for flashing and leveling.
- Grind edges clean and prime bare metal quickly.
- Test-fit the steel reinforcement frame before final fastening.
- Keep the opening square so standard windows install cleanly.

## 4. Prevent rust and leaks

- Prime all cut edges.
- Use exterior sealant compatible with steel and window flashing materials.
- Flash the sill so water sheds out, not inward.
- Add a drip cap or head flashing above the window in exposed weather.

## 5. Add wood framing the right way

- Do not rely on thin container skin alone to hold interior walls.
- Use welded steel tabs, rivnuts, or anchored furring strips to create attachment points.
- Pressure-treated lumber is useful where condensation risk is high, but isolate wood from wet steel where possible.
- Typical interior framing uses vertical wood furring or a light stud wall set just off the steel skin.

## 6. Leave room for insulation and services

- Plan wall depth around insulation, wiring, plumbing, and interior finish.
- Closed-cell spray foam is common because it controls condensation and seals gaps.
- If using batt or board insulation, include a condensation strategy and thermal break.

## 7. Window install sequence

1. Mark and cut the opening.
2. Weld or fasten reinforcement frame.
3. Prime and seal exposed metal.
4. Install sill flashing and weather protection.
5. Set the window square and level.
6. Fasten to the reinforced framing, not weak sheet steel alone.
7. Seal, flash, and test with water.

## 8. Wood framing layout inside the container

- Furring-only layout: fastest, best when interior loads are light.
- Full stud wall: better for wiring, plumbing, cabinets, and thicker insulation.
- Hybrid layout: welded steel frame at openings plus wood framing elsewhere.

## 9. Good practice notes

- Keep a rust-treatment kit on hand during all cutting and grinding work.
- Avoid too many large openings on one side without checking structure.
- Think about egress, ventilation, and solar heat gain before placing windows.
- In hot climates, shade and overhangs matter almost as much as the window itself.
